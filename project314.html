The Discovery

Pi hidden in the Exodus Tabernacle

In 2014, engineer Andrew Hoy made a discovery that has been hidden inside the Hebrew Bible for over three thousand years. While studying the Exodus Tabernacle text, he found the most accurate approximation of Pi (π ≈ 3.14) known to the ancient world, accurate to within 0.05%, encoded directly in Exodus chapters 26 and 27.

The curtain dimensions of the Tabernacle, when joined correctly according to the literal Hebrew instructions, produce a cylindrical assembly measuring exactly 314 cubits in circumference, a near-perfect multiple of π × 100. This is not a numerical coincidence. This is the architectural key that unlocks the entire Exodus Tabernacle design.

The Tabernacle courtyard was not a rectangle. It was a circle, a round, domed, yurt-like structure towering six storeys above the wilderness ground. And it has been lost in translation for over two thousand years.

The Evidence

The Tabernacle Diagrams

Traditional Model, Assumed

The Rectangular Assembly

The traditional interpretation joins curtains long-edge to long-edge, producing a flat rectangular swatch. This approach leaves two opposing edges unconnected, directly violating the Exodus text which specifies loops on both edges of every sheet.

Project 314, Biblical Model

The Cylindrical Assembly

When joined short-edge to short-edge, exactly as the Hebrew specifies, the eleven curtains form a cylinder. All loop joints are connected. The resulting circumference: 314 cubits. π × 100. The Tabernacle courtyard is a perfect circle.

The Mathematical Key

314 Cubits, The Pi Constant in Moses' Tabernacle

The circumference of the Tabernacle courtyard amounts to 314 cubits, a near-perfect multiple of the mathematical constant π (Pi). This number, embedded in the very fabric measurements of the Exodus text, is the "Rosetta Stone" of the entire Tabernacle design. Once you see it, every other detail of the Exodus account resolves into coherence.

Two Options

Two Joining Methods, Two Completely Different Structures

A single decision, which edges to join, produces either a flat rectangle or a cylinder. Two shapes. Two applications. Two completely different Tabernacles. The Hebrew text, read literally, specifies only one.

Not a theologian. An engineer.

Andrew Hoy does not hold a degree in theology. He is a professional engineer, and it was precisely his engineering mindset, his habit of drawing free body diagrams and following specifications to the letter, that led him to see what generations of biblical scholars had missed.

He did not set out to make a discovery. He was not even studying the Tabernacle with particular interest. In 2014, following what he describes as a divine prompting, he sat down with the Hebrew Exodus text, "put his engineering hat on," and did what his father, also an engineer and professor, had taught him: draw a free body diagram with tender loving care. Use only what the text actually says. Add nothing of your own.

What he found changed everything he thought he knew about the house of God.

The Discovery

As he carefully inventoried every measurement in the Exodus curtain texts, Hoy noticed that the eleven goat-hair sheets, joined short-edge to short-edge as the Hebrew literally instructs, produced a cylindrical assembly. And the circumference of that cylinder: 314 cubits.

Pi. Encoded in the fabric of Moses' tent. To within 0.05% accuracy. The most precise ancient-world approximation of the constant known to exist, more accurate than the Egyptians, the Babylonians, and the early Greeks. And it had been sitting in plain sight in the Hebrew Bible for three thousand five hundred years.

"It is not by accident," Hoy writes, "that there is more Hebrew text describing the Tabernacle tent fabric than there is describing the Ark of the Covenant. The tent curtains weren't made first and foremost for decorative purposes. The secret to the Tabernacle design was never meant to be covered; the Tabernacle's secret is the covering, hidden in plain sight."

The Journey

Hoy launched Project 314 on 3/14/15, the Pi Day of the century, expecting the discovery to propagate rapidly through social media and religious communities. It did not. What followed instead was years of patient, determined work: writing, drawing, presenting, and engaging scholars, rabbis, engineers, and ordinary believers who were willing to test everything for themselves.

The Resistance

Hoy is candid about the reception. Most institutional experts, he found, are either unable or unwilling to engage the discovery seriously. Some lack the technical training. Some fear the implications for their careers. Some defer automatically to tradition. The pattern is familiar, as he notes, even the returning exiles of Ezra's day, fully funded and liberated for the task, refrained from building God's house for over a decade.

"I'm not trying to kiss the back of the hand of some shadowy Godfather-esque figure to get permission to operate in his jurisdiction," Hoy writes. "The research stands upon the authority of the word from which it is derived."

To date, Hoy reports, no one has formally challenged his exegesis in debate. Those who engage him technically either concede the point or fall silent. The Hebrew text, examined without preconceived assumptions, tells only one story: God's Tabernacle was round.
