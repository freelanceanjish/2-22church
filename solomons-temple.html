Solomon's Temple

Why the shoebox museum model fails — and what circular dome geometry proposes from Scripture and Ezekiel's vision.

Solomon built before Babylon taught Israel to love bricks. Why would his house look like the gate that came later?

Section I
Traditional vs Contextual

Why the shoebox model fails

Museum models, Sunday-school diagrams, and Josephus-influenced reconstructions show Solomon's house as a flat-roofed rectangular hall — a stone shoebox. That is the traditional mainstream design. We believe it is largely a backward projection of Second Temple and Babylonian gate architecture onto a building completed centuries earlier.

Traditional — fails

Rectangular shoebox temple

Flat walls, flat roof, Babylon-influenced proportions read backward into 959 BC.

Contextual — holds

Circular plan with central dome

Twenty-cubit diameter, oracle cube at centre, molten sea declaring pi at the threshold.

How traditional diagrams fail

  • Babylonian transposition — After exile, Israel rebuilt among brick monumentalism. Second Temple trends 60×60 (squarer). Elders wept (Ezra 3:12) — they remembered a different form, not only size.
  • Ignores molten sea — A ten-cubit diameter, thirty-cubit circumference vessel sits at the temple threshold (1 Kings 7:23). Tradition treats it as furniture; context treats it as architectural key — pi at the door.
  • Flattens Ezekiel's circle — Ezekiel 40–48 and Henry Sulley's 1887 model describe circular enclosure, four towers, central throne — not a shoebox.
  • Breaks pattern continuity — Ark shell → tabernacle decagon → Solomon → heavenly city. Only the shoebox model breaks the curve.
Transposition error. Post-exilic brick gate logic read backward onto pre-exilic cedar and stone.
Section II

What Scripture records

1 Kings 6:2–3 · 7:23 (KJV)
The house… threescore cubits long, twenty broad, thirty high… And he made a molten sea, ten cubits from brim to brim, round… and a line of thirty cubits did compass it round about.

Porch breadth equals house breadth (twenty). In circular reading, twenty = diameter, sixty = outer perimeter. Sulley's Temple of Ezekiel's Prophecy (1887) and Thomas Newberry's dome models show this was not invented yesterday.
