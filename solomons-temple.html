959 BC · First Temple

Solomon's Temple

Not a shoebox imposed by Babylonian memory. A circular dwelling with dome, shadowing Ezekiel's vision as Henry Sulley drew it.

I doubted the shoe-box temples in my heart long before I wrote a word. Sunflowers, pinecones, ferns: the Creator wove holy geometry into creation. Why would His house look like a Babylonian brick when His patterns curve through everything He made?

First Temple vs Second Temple

Solomon's Temple was completed around 959 BC (1 Kings 6:38). It was built of hewn stone and cedar before Israel ever saw Babylon's Ishtar Gate (c. 575 BC). After exile, elders wept when the Second Temple foundation was laid — not only because it looked poorer, but because the form had changed (Ezra 3:12; Haggai 2:3).

Scripture records Solomon's house as 60 × 20 × 30 cubits. The Second Temple platform measurements trend toward a 60 × 60 cubit footprint in later descriptions — squarer, more monumental, more Babylonian. We believe centuries of exile transposed brick-box assumptions onto Solomon's earlier pattern.

What if the house David's son built was circular under its cedar, with a dome catching light the way Ezekiel later saw heaven opened?

Primary Text

What the kings recorded

1 Kings 6:2–3 (KJV)
And the house which king Solomon built for the Lord, the length thereof was threescore cubits, and the breadth thereof twenty cubits, and the height thereof thirty cubits. And the porch before the temple of the house, twenty cubits was the length thereof, according to the breadth of the house; and ten cubits was the breadth thereof before the house.

The porch breadth equals the house breadth (twenty cubits). The porch length equals the house length (sixty cubits). In a circular reading, sixty cubits may describe circumference or perimeter segments, while twenty cubits marks diameter — the same way tabernacle measurements encode pi when read in context (see God of Pi).

Henry Sulley · 1887

Ezekiel's vision made visible

Henry Sulley published The Temple of Ezekiel's Prophecy in 1887, attempting to render Ezekiel 40–48 as a unified architectural model. His drawing places a great circular enclosure with four outer towers evoking the four living creatures, twenty-four inner chambers echoing the elders, and a central throne area where sacrifice points to the Lamb.

Sulley was not alone. Thomas Newberry (1831–1901) produced a Solomon's Temple model with a prominent central dome decades earlier. These were British scholars working from Scripture before modern "nonlinear architecture" language existed.

We do not treat any Victorian model as infallible. We treat them as proof that serious readers already saw curvature in the text before Hollywood and Sunday-school flannelgraphs flattened everything into rectangles.

Arithmetic

Numbers in the pattern

Rectangular volume (traditional reading)

60 × 20 × 30 = 36,000 cubic cubits internal volume (simplified).

Floor footprint = 60 × 20 = 1,200 square cubits.

Circular reading (diameter = 20 cubits)

Radius = 10 cubits. Floor area = π × 10² ≈ 314.16 square cubits — pi again.

If outer perimeter ring totals sixty cubits of arc around a decagonal shell, each face ≈ 6 cubits — linking Solomon's house to the dome decagon pattern of the wilderness tabernacle.

Molten sea: diameter 10, circumference 30 → ratio 30 ÷ 10 = 3 (biblical approximation of π), placed at the threshold between court and house.

First vs Second Temple (Ezra-era contrast)

Solomon: 60 × 20 × 30 — elongated footprint, lower relative width-to-height.

Second Temple descriptions trending 60 × 60 — three times the width in the squarer dimension, consistent with post-exilic monumental gates and flat platforms.
