Solomon's First Temple

Solomon's Temple

I grew up with the flat-roofed rectangle on every museum wall. Solomon built in cedar and stone before Babylon taught Israel to love bricks. That mismatch would not leave me alone.

Solomon built before Babylon taught Israel to love bricks. Why would his house look like the gate that came later?

Start here

The temple you know, and why it breaks

Every museum model I have seen shows the same thing: a flat-roofed rectangle. Stone walls. A long hall. Sometimes a little porch stuck on the front like an afterthought. Sunday-school felt boards use the same shape. I accepted it for years because it was everywhere.

Then I read 1 Kings 6 without the diagram in my head, and a question would not leave me: Solomon finished this house in 959 BC. Babylon's brick gates came later. The Second Temple came later. Why does every picture of Solomon's house look like something built after the exile?

What we were shown

The stone shoebox

Flat walls, flat roof, proportions that smell of Babylon read backward into cedar and stone.

What I think fits

A circular dwelling with dome

Twenty cubits across. Oracle cube at the centre. Molten sea at the threshold declaring pi before you enter.

Here is what troubled me about the shoebox, piece by piece.

After the exile Israel rebuilt among Babylon's brick monumentalism and the Second Temple trended squarer at sixty by sixty, and when the foundation was laid the elders wept (Ezra 3:12), where I used to think they wept because the house was smaller but now I wonder if they wept because the shape was wrong and they remembered something the new drawings had already flattened.

Then there is the molten sea, ten cubits across and thirty cubits around the brim, sitting at the threshold of the house (1 Kings 7:23) while tradition treats it like furniture or a fancy washbasin, though ten and thirty are not decoration but pi at the door, and I keep asking why pi would greet you at the entrance of a flat rectangle that never needed circumference.

Ezekiel's vision does not help the shoebox either, because chapters 40 through 48 describe a circular enclosure with four towers and a throne above the firmament, which Henry Sulley drew in 1887 and Thomas Newberry built into dome models, so I am not the first man to look at Solomon's numbers and hear a circle.

The pattern troubled me too, from ark shell through tabernacle decagon to Solomon's house and the heavenly city, where only the museum model breaks the curve and that bothered me more than I expected.

I think we took post-exilic gate logic and laid it over pre-exilic cedar, because the brick came later and the drawing should have asked when.
The text itself

What Kings actually records

1 Kings 6:2, 3 · 7:23 (KJV)
The house… threescore cubits long, twenty broad, thirty high… And he made a molten sea, ten cubits from brim to brim, round… and a line of thirty cubits did compass it round about.

The porch breadth equals the house breadth at both twenty, and I sat with that for a long time because in every shoebox drawing twenty is just a width while in a circular reading twenty is the diameter and sixty is the outer perimeter, and I am not forcing pi where it does not belong since the molten sea already puts it at the door.

Sulley's Temple of Ezekiel's Prophecy (1887) and Newberry's dome models showed me I was not alone in hearing a circle in these verses. Men before me read the same numbers and drew something curved overhead. That gave me courage to keep going.

My best reading

What I think Solomon built

Here is where I landed, with a circular dwelling and a dome overhead, the oracle or most holy place as a twenty-cubit cube at the centre, and the molten sea at the entry round at ten across and thirty around so pi greets you before you cross the threshold.

It fits Ezekiel's enthroned firmament vision in my chest, even if I cannot prove every cubit from Kings alone. It fits the tabernacle work I learned from Project314.org, pi already hidden in the wilderness tent. And it fits the arc of structures across Scripture: curve, circumference, breath, not flat industrial boxes.

Researchers at Project Arc sharpened my thinking here. I cite them. The voice is mine.

Sit with the cubits

The arithmetic that kept me honest

I worked these on paper the way I did with the ark. Not to prove God. To find out whether the numbers could carry what Scripture was saying.

If you treat it like a rectangle

60 × 20 × 30 = 36,000 cubic cubits inside. Footprint on the ground: 1,200 square cubits. That is the shoebox math every diagram uses.

If twenty cubits is the diameter

Radius = 10. Base area = π × 10² ≈ 314.16 square cubits. The molten sea: 30 ÷ 10 = 3, pi at the threshold. The oracle cube: 20³ = 8,000 cubic cubits at the holy centre.

Those numbers did not settle every debate for me. They only showed the shoebox was not the only honest reading of what Kings wrote.
