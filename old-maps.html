Historical Evidence

Old Maps

Historical cartography from the 17th and 18th centuries showing geographical features that raise significant questions about the standard modern cosmological narrative. These are primary source documents held in public archives.

The maps collected here come from institutional archives including the David Rumsey Map Collection, the Internet Archive and rare map dealers. They are relevant because several of them depict geographical features, including landmasses, coastlines and boundaries, that do not fit comfortably into the currently accepted model of world geography.

Notable among these are the Terre de Iesso maps — a series of 17th and 18th century French charts showing a large landmass north of Japan that does not correspond to any landmass in modern geography. Philippe Buache's 1754 charts are among the most detailed. The 1688 Coronelli map of America Settentrionale similarly shows geographical features and boundaries that have provoked serious discussion among researchers.

We present these as primary sources for your own study. Draw your own conclusions.

Buache — Terre de Iesso 1754

David Rumsey Map Collection

Philippe Buache (1700–1773) was the official Geographer to the King of France. His 1754 series of charts documenting the coastlines of Terre de Iesso — a large landmass believed to exist north of Japan — is among the most detailed historical cartography of a region that does not appear in modern world geography. These charts are held in the David Rumsey Map Collection, one of the world's largest online collections of historical cartography.
