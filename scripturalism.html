Biblical Epistemology

The Word Judges History.
Not the Other Way Around.

On Scripturalism, the authority of God's Word, and why the Church has it backwards.

Doctrine Discernment May 2026

There is a problem in the Church today, and it is not what most people think it is. It is not a lack of Bible teaching, or a shortage of conferences, or too few commentary series. The problem is that we have quietly handed the gavel to the wrong party. Scholars, teachers, and theologians have spent the better part of two centuries placing the historical record above the Word of God, and they have done it with such academic confidence that most believers never noticed the swap. They dressed the surrender in the language of intellectual rigour and called it mature faith. It is neither.

Think about what actually happens when external data — archaeological findings, carbon-dated timelines, ancient Near Eastern manuscripts — becomes the measuring rod by which Scripture is tested. You have not added helpful context. You have installed a higher court. The creature is now holding the gavel that belongs to the Creator, and the Creator is in the dock waiting for a verdict.

Think about it honestly

If a judge walked into a courtroom and let the defendant set the rules of evidence, determine what counts as proof, and decide which testimonies are credible — would you call that justice? Because that is exactly what happens when Scripture is made to stand before the bar of secular scholarship and wait for approval. Someone in that room has been appointed judge. Who is it?

✦

The Apostles Never Did This

When Peter stood up on the day of Pentecost, he did not open with a bibliography. He did not footnote Josephus, defer to Roman historians, or reassure the crowd that his account had been cross-referenced against the available archaeological consensus. He proclaimed what he knew — and three thousand people were converted before lunchtime. Whatever power was operating in that moment, it was clearly not dependent on the world's permission.

The Apostles had witnessed something. They wrote about what they witnessed. And the authority behind their testimony was not borrowed from Rome or Greece or the rabbinical establishment — it came from the Word that had spoken into creation itself. The modern impulse to validate Scripture through external sources is not intellectual maturity; it is a faith that has quietly agreed to sit in the defendant's chair and wait for the world to tell it whether it can stand up.

Scripture is the axiom. It is the self-authenticating foundation. You cannot justify it by appealing to something outside it without making that external thing the higher authority.

Gordon H. Clark  ·  Scripturalist Epistemology

Clark's point cuts right to it. Every system of thought has a foundation it cannot prove from something prior to itself — that is just what a foundation is. The secular scholar's foundation is reason and empirical method, which he trusts without being able to prove they are trustworthy from something outside them. The Christian's foundation is Scripture. The moment you place archaeology, or scientific consensus, or peer-reviewed journals in the seat of judge over the Word, you have not strengthened your faith. You have replaced its foundation. And you have probably not noticed you did it, which is the most dangerous part.

Worth sitting with

What is your starting point, the thing you trust before you trust anything else? If you say "evidence," who taught you that evidence is reliable? If you say "reason," who validated reason? Every thinker, whether they admit it or not, stands on something they cannot prove from below. The Christian's ground is Scripture. What is the secular scholar's — and why does his unprovable foundation get to judge yours?

✦

How We Got Here — And What It Is Costing Us

The way it happened is almost too simple. The more external sources a teacher can cite, the more credible he appears in academic circles, and since the Church has been chasing the approval of academic circles for about two hundred years, the incentives all pointed in the wrong direction. Teachers learned to open with the archaeology and arrive at the text later, if at all. The Word of God became the thing you arrived at after establishing your secular credentials, rather than the thing you started with.

And once that inversion is in place, the costs are not trivial. The biblical timeline gets bent to fit secular dating systems. The Genesis account gets softened to accommodate evolutionary frameworks. Revelation gets reinterpreted through the lens of Roman political history, as if the primary task of the text is to make sense to a first-century Roman governor rather than to speak with authority across all of history. We end up doing archaeology with a Bible we occasionally glance at, and calling it exegesis.

The real damage is not to academic credibility. It is to the people in the pews who are being taught, week after week, that the Word of God needs external sources to stand up straight.

Be honest with yourself

Has the teacher or pastor you respect most ever said, in effect: "The Bible seems to say this, but the scholars think that"? Did you catch, in that moment, who just got handed the higher authority? And when the scholars change their minds — which they do, regularly, sometimes dramatically — does the Word of God change with them? Because if it does, you are not reading Scripture. You are reading the current scholarly consensus with some verses attached.

✦

Reverse the Order. Everything Changes.

The Scripturalist position is simply the correct inversion of what is currently practised. You do not test the Word of God against the historical record. You test the historical record against the Word of God. External sources are not excluded — they are brought in as witnesses, not judges. You examine them by the standard Scripture establishes, and where they resist it, you do not immediately conclude that Scripture is wrong. You ask where the secular presuppositions were smuggled into the method.

Take carbon dating as an example, since it comes up every time someone wants to put the Genesis timeline on trial. Carbon dating, geological uniformitarianism, and evolutionary timelines all begin from a common assumption: that conditions on earth have been essentially stable over vast periods of time, and that there was no catastrophic global event that reset the baseline. In other words, they begin by assuming the flood did not happen, and then they present their results as if those results are independent of that assumption. When their numbers contradict Genesis, this is presented as "the science" — but it is really just the original assumption returning its own echo. You put in "no flood," you get back "no flood." That is not a refutation of Scripture. That is a circular argument with a very expensive laboratory attached to it.

The Bible is the supreme authority in all matters of faith and practice, and it alone interprets history, not the other way around.

Armando Ballart  ·  The End Is Behind Us
✦

The Millennium, the Little Season, and a Church Reading the Wrong Map

Nowhere does the cost of the inverted approach show up more painfully than in eschatology, which is ironic given how much energy the Church has spent on it. The dominant prophetic systems — Premillennialism, Dispensationalism, and their various popular offspring — did not emerge from someone sitting quietly with Revelation and asking what the text actually says. They emerged in a context where nineteenth-century European historical assumptions were already shaping how prophecy was being read, and where certain publishing houses and Bible conference circuits were very effective at distributing a particular framework before anyone had time to examine its foundations.

Armando Ballart's work applies Scripturalist method directly to this. The question he presses is precise and uncomfortable: what if the prophetic timeline that nearly all of Western Christianity has inherited is not derived from Scripture, but imposed upon it? What if the Millennium is not ahead of us but behind us, and we are already in Satan's little season — the short time of his loosing described in Revelation 20 — and we cannot see it because we keep reading the text through a grid built from assumptions the text itself never established?

That is not a fringe position. It is a disciplined, Scripture-first argument that takes the text seriously enough to ask whether what we were handed in Sunday school actually came from the Bible, or came from Darby's system, Scofield's notes, and a century of prophecy conferences that kept citing each other.

A question worth following all the way down

You were taught a prophetic timeline. But who taught the people who taught you, and what were they reading when they built it? If you traced it back honestly — through your pastor, through his seminary, through the commentaries he trusted — would you arrive at a plain reading of the text, or at a nineteenth-century theological system that was already working from external historical assumptions? And if you found out the map was wrong, would you have the courage to follow the text to wherever it actually leads, even if it meant everything you were taught about "the end times" needs to be rebuilt from the ground up?

✦

What Scripturalism Actually Demands

Let me be clear about what this position requires, because it is not the comfortable option. It means accepting that mainstream scholarship — including mainstream Christian scholarship — will regularly arrive at conclusions that Scripture, honestly handled, contradicts. It means having the nerve to say the external record has been misread rather than that Scripture is wrong, and then doing the actual work of showing where and how the misreading occurred. Gordon Clark was emphatic on this: Scripturalism is a rival epistemology, not a retreat from rigorous thought. You cannot just wave your hand at the evidence and call it secular bias. You have to engage it, examine its foundations, and show where the presuppositions are doing the work that is being credited to the data.

The call is not to ignore the world's scholarship. It is to stop letting it sit in the judge's seat. Bring it in as a witness. Ask it hard questions. Cross-examine it against the Word. And be willing to let the Word render a verdict that contradicts centuries of received wisdom, because if you are only willing to follow the text when it agrees with what you already believed, you are not following the text at all.

The Church does not need more scholars brave enough to question Scripture. It needs more believers brave enough to question the scholars.

One question to close with

If Scripture is true — and you say you believe it is — then why does it need the world's permission to say so? What would actually change in how you read the Bible tomorrow if you decided, once and for all, that it answers to no other court?

The Word of God is not a document waiting to be verified by the next round of excavations. It is the standard by which everything else gets verified. When the Church recovers that conviction — not as a slogan to feel good about, but as an actual method applied with rigour and without apology — it will find that Scripture does not merely survive the scrutiny of history. It explains it.
