Biblical Epistemology

Scripturalism:
The Word Judges History

I spent years letting history judge the Bible instead of the other way around. This is what it cost me, and what I think Scripture actually demands.

Doctrine Discernment May 2026

A visitor once walked through a botanical garden and stopped at a banyan tree. He stared at it for a long moment, counted the thick columns rising from the ground, and then turned to the gardener with genuine confidence: "How many stumps does this tree need to hold itself up? I have never seen anything quite like it."

The gardener looked at him. Then at the tree. Then back at him.

"Those are not stumps."

The visitor blinked. "Sorry?"

"Those are branches. The tree grew them downward until they touched the soil and took root. They did not come from below to support the tree. They came from the tree itself, reaching down. It is the tree that supports them. You have the direction backwards."

The visitor looked again. The whole thing reorganised in front of him.

Then the gardener pointed to a few thicker growths near the outer edge. "Those ones there, though. Those are not part of this tree at all. Different root system entirely. They simply grew close enough to look like they belonged." He let that sit for a moment. "They will be cut off soon enough."

I keep coming back to that banyan image when I watch how the Church handles Scripture today. For two hundred years we have been bolting external sources onto the Word: archaeology, carbon dating, Near Eastern manuscripts, secular historiography, telling ourselves these things support the Bible and help it stand up in the modern world. But here is what we forget, honestly: those sources did not arrive clean. History is not a neutral witness sitting quietly on the shelf. It is the most edited, selectively preserved, politically managed pile of material in human existence, and every era that touched it had a reason to shape it. Take that and use it as the lens for reading Scripture? That is not scholarship. That is putting on someone else's glasses and then wondering why the Word looks blurry.

Think about it honestly

The visitor had the direction wrong, so of course his whole reading of the tree was wrong; at least the tree was real and standing in front of him. Now look at what we do with history: written by winners, rewritten by the next winners, preserved selectively, edited politically, sometimes fabricated outright. Empires have always shaped the record to serve themselves. What we call "established history" is mostly what survived the fires, the purges, the translations, and the agendas of whoever held power. We take that and use it as the measuring rod for the Word of God. We hand the gavel to the most tampered document in human existence and ask it to rule on the most reliable one.

✦

The Apostles Never Did This

When Peter stood up on Pentecost he did not open with a bibliography. No footnotes to Josephus, no deferral to Roman historians, no reassurance that his account had been cross-referenced against the archaeological consensus. He proclaimed what he knew. Three thousand people were converted before lunchtime. Whatever power was in that room, it was not waiting on the world's permission.

The Apostles witnessed something and wrote it down; look where their authority did not come from. Not Rome, not Greece, not the rabbinical schools. What stood behind their testimony was the Word that had spoken creation into being. When we feel we must validate Scripture through external sources before we can trust it, I do not think that is intellectual maturity. I think it is a faith that has quietly agreed to sit in the defendant's chair and wait for the world to tell it whether it can stand.

Scripture is the axiom. It is the self-authenticating foundation. You cannot justify it by appealing to something outside it without making that external thing the higher authority.

Gordon H. Clark  ·  Scripturalist Epistemology

Clark's point cuts right to it. Every system of thought has a foundation it cannot prove from something prior to itself; that is what a foundation is. The secular scholar trusts reason and empirical method without being able to prove, from outside them, that they are trustworthy. The Christian's foundation is Scripture. Place archaeology, scientific consensus, or peer-reviewed journals in the judge's seat over the Word and you have not strengthened your faith; you have replaced its foundation, probably without noticing, which is the most dangerous part.

Worth sitting with

What is your starting point, the thing you trust before you trust anything else? If you say "evidence," who taught you that evidence is reliable? If you say "reason," who validated reason? Every thinker, whether they admit it or not, stands on something they cannot prove from below. The Christian's ground is Scripture. What is the secular scholar's? And why does his unprovable foundation get to judge yours?

✦

How We Got Here, and What It Is Costing Us

The way we got here is almost too simple. The more external sources a teacher can cite, the more credible he looks in academic circles, and the Church has been chasing that approval for about two hundred years, so the incentives all pointed the wrong way. Teachers learned to open with archaeology and arrive at the text later, if at all. The Word of God became something you reached after establishing your secular credentials, not the place you started.

Once that inversion is in place the costs are real. The biblical timeline gets bent to fit secular dating. Genesis gets softened for evolutionary frameworks. Revelation gets read through Roman political history, as if the text's main job is to make sense to a first-century governor rather than to speak with authority across all of history. We end up doing archaeology with a Bible we occasionally glance at and calling it exegesis.

The real damage is not to academic credibility. It is to the people in the pews who are being taught, week after week, that the Word of God needs external sources to stand up straight.

Be honest with yourself

Has the teacher or pastor you respect most ever said, in effect: "The Bible seems to say this, but the scholars think that"? Did you catch, in that moment, who just got handed the higher authority? And when the scholars change their minds (and they do, regularly, sometimes dramatically), does the Word of God change with them? Because if it does, you are not reading Scripture. You are reading the current scholarly consensus with some verses attached.

✦

Reverse the Order. Everything Changes.

The Scripturalist position is simply the correct inversion of what is currently practised. You do not test the Word of God against the historical record. You test the historical record against the Word of God. External sources are not excluded; they are brought in as witnesses, not judges. You examine them by the standard Scripture establishes, and where they resist it, you do not immediately conclude that Scripture is wrong. You ask where the secular presuppositions were smuggled into the method.

Take carbon dating, since it comes up every time someone wants to put Genesis on trial. Carbon dating, geological uniformitarianism, evolutionary timelines: they all begin from the same assumption, that conditions on earth have been essentially stable for vast ages and that no catastrophic global flood reset the baseline. They assume the flood did not happen, then present their results as if those results were independent of that assumption. When the numbers contradict Genesis we are told that is "the science," when really it is the original assumption returning its own echo. You put in "no flood," you get back "no flood." That is not a refutation of Scripture; it is a circular argument with a very expensive laboratory attached.

The Bible is the supreme authority in all matters of faith and practice, and it alone interprets history, not the other way around.

Armando Ballart  ·  The End Is Behind Us
✦

The Millennium, the Little Season, and a Church Reading the Wrong Map

Nowhere does the cost of the inverted approach show up more painfully than in eschatology, which is ironic given how much energy the Church has spent on it. The dominant prophetic systems (Premillennialism, Dispensationalism, and their various popular offspring) did not emerge from someone sitting quietly with Revelation and asking what the text actually says. They emerged in a context where nineteenth-century European historical assumptions were already shaping how prophecy was being read, and where certain publishing houses and Bible conference circuits were very effective at distributing a particular framework before anyone had time to examine its foundations.

Armando Ballart's work applies Scripturalist method directly to this. The question he presses is precise and uncomfortable: what if the prophetic timeline that nearly all of Western Christianity has inherited is not derived from Scripture, but imposed upon it? What if the Millennium is not ahead of us but behind us, and we are already in Satan's little season, the short time of his loosing described in Revelation 20, and we cannot see it because we keep reading the text through a grid built from assumptions the text itself never established?

I do not think that is a fringe position. It is a disciplined, Scripture-first argument that takes the text seriously enough to ask whether what we were handed in Sunday school actually came from the Bible, or from Darby's system, Scofield's notes, and a century of prophecy conferences citing each other.

A question worth following all the way down

You were taught a prophetic timeline. But who taught the people who taught you, and what were they reading when they built it? If you traced it back honestly, through your pastor, through his seminary, through the commentaries he trusted, would you arrive at a plain reading of the text, or at a nineteenth-century theological system that was already working from external historical assumptions? And if you found out the map was wrong, would you have the courage to follow the text to wherever it actually leads, even if it meant everything you were taught about "the end times" needs to be rebuilt from the ground up?

✦

What Scripturalism Actually Demands

Let me be clear about what this position requires, because it is not the comfortable option. It means accepting that mainstream scholarship (including mainstream Christian scholarship) will regularly arrive at conclusions that Scripture, honestly handled, contradicts. It means having the nerve to say the external record has been misread rather than that Scripture is wrong, and then doing the actual work of showing where and how the misreading occurred. Gordon Clark was emphatic on this: Scripturalism is a rival epistemology, not a retreat from rigorous thought. You cannot just wave your hand at the evidence and call it secular bias. You have to engage it, examine its foundations, and show where the presuppositions are doing the work that is being credited to the data.

The call is not to ignore the world's scholarship. Stop letting it sit in the judge's seat. Bring it in as a witness, ask it hard questions, cross-examine it against the Word, and be willing to let the Word render a verdict that contradicts centuries of received wisdom; if you only follow the text when it agrees with what you already believed, you are not following the text at all.

The Church does not need more scholars brave enough to question Scripture. It needs more believers brave enough to question the scholars.

One question to close with

If Scripture is true, and you say you believe it is, then why does it need the world's permission to say so? What would actually change in how you read the Bible tomorrow if you decided, once and for all, that it answers to no other court?

The Word of God is not a document waiting to be verified by the next round of excavations. It is the standard by which everything else gets verified. When the Church recovers that conviction, not as a slogan but as an actual method applied with rigour, Scripture does not merely survive the scrutiny of history. It explains it. That is the order. That is where we start.
